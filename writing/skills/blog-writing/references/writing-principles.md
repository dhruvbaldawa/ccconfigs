# Writing Principles — Source References

Compiled sources behind the Stage-Setting beat, Structural Principles, and Quality Checklist additions in `SKILL.md`. Use this document to pull direct quotes when editing or to decide edge cases.

Each principle: one-line statement, why it matters, how to apply, source.

---

## 1. Curse of Knowledge

**Principle:** The writer's knowledge makes it hard to imagine what the reader doesn't know. Skipped scaffolding is the dominant failure mode in expert-written prose.

**Why it matters:** You stop seeing the gaps because you've closed them. The reader hits undefined terms, assumed context, and implicit dependencies—and quietly drops off.

**How to apply:** Read each draft as a newcomer. For any technical term, ask: "Would a smart general reader know this?" If no, prime it at first use.

**Sources:**
- Steven Pinker, *The Sense of Style* (2014), Ch. 1–3
- Chip & Dan Heath, *Made to Stick* (2007)—chapter on the curse of knowledge with the "tappers and listeners" study

---

## 2. Reader-Centric Framing (Horizontal vs. Vertical)

**Principle:** Writers use text to think *horizontally*—a record of their process. Readers consume text *vertically*—value delivered. Drafts fail when they narrate the writer's figuring-out instead of answering the reader's question.

**Why it matters:** Every community has codes—words and phrases that signal "I understand your values." Value is what the reader takes away, not what the writer poured in.

**How to apply:** Before drafting, define: what problem does my reader face? What do they value? The intro answers those before the argument begins.

**Source:** Larry McEnerney, "The Craft of Writing Effectively," University of Chicago Leadership Lab lecture. Available on YouTube (~90 min). The horizontal/vertical distinction appears ~18:00–25:00.

## 3. SCQA Structure

**Principle:** Ground the reader with Situation → Complication → Question → Answer. Situation = the agreed baseline. Complication = what changed. Question = what naturally emerges. Answer = your argument.

**Why it matters:** Skipping "Situation" is the common defect—drafts jump to the Complication or the Answer before the reader knows the baseline. SCQA is the named framework for stage-setting.

**How to apply:** Before writing each section, write one sentence for each element. If you can't, the section isn't grounded yet.

**Source:** Barbara Minto, *The Pyramid Principle* (1973; revised 2020). Originally developed at McKinsey for consulting memos, widely applicable to essay openings.

## 4. Hook + Stakes + Skepticism

**Principle:** The intro must do two jobs: hook the reader, and prove the promise. Readers arrive skeptical—name the problem they face, tease novelty, combat their doubts before they tune out.

**Why it matters:** A hook without stakes lands but doesn't hold. A promise without proof gets skimmed and abandoned.

**How to apply:** Write the intro first. Show it to two target readers alone. Ask: "After reading only this, what questions do you most want answered?" If their questions don't excite you, rewrite.

**Source:** Julian Shapiro, "Writing Handbook" — julian.com/guide/write (section on hooks and intro structure).

## 5. Progressive Disclosure (in Prose)

**Principle:** Layer information by cognitive load. Analogy → problem → core concept → details → depth. Define terms at the point of first use, never via glossary or forward reference.

**Why it matters:** Cognitive load spikes when readers meet unfamiliar terms without context. Glossaries make it worse—they force flipping. Progressive disclosure is a UX principle (Nielsen Norman) that applies to written prose just as well.

**How to apply:** For any technical concept, write two versions: (1) one-sentence plain-English, (2) technical. Lead with (1). A Cisco Fellow's rule: "Explain it in a way my mum would understand first, then dive into the detail."

**Sources:**
- Interaction Design Foundation, "Progressive Disclosure" — https://www.interaction-design.org/literature/topics/progressive-disclosure
- Julia Evans, "How I write useful programming comics" — https://jvns.ca/blog/2020/12/05/how-i-write-useful-programming-comics/
- Ralf Wolter / PowerSpeaking, technical-presentation guidance (2022)

## 6. Surprise Before Argument

**Principle:** Lead with what contradicts the reader's assumption, not with what you want to convince them of. Curiosity before case.

**Why it matters:** Paul Graham: "Surprises are things that you not only didn't know, but that contradict things you thought you knew. And so they're the most valuable sort of fact you can get." People retain surprising facts longer than arguments.

**How to apply:** Structure: "You probably think X. Actually, Y. Here's why." If the draft opens with "I want to argue Y," rewrite.

**Source:** Paul Graham, "The Age of the Essay" (2004) — https://paulgraham.com/essay.html. Also "Good Writing" (2025) — https://paulgraham.com/goodwriting.html.

## 7. Outcome Before Mechanism

**Principle:** State what the reader will understand or be able to do, then explain how it works. Don't teach foundations first.

**Why it matters:** The reader needs to know why the mechanics are worth learning. Outcome-first orients them; mechanism-first loses them.

**How to apply:** Julia Evans' pattern: "Here's a surprising fact you didn't know [outcome], which explains why X happens [mechanism], which matters because [consequence]."

**Source:** Julia Evans, Wizard Zines — https://wizardzines.com. Also jvns.ca essays on teaching technical concepts.

---

## 8. Concrete Before Abstract

**Principle:** Real scenario first, extracted principle second. The example is not decoration—it's the load-bearing wall.

**Why it matters:** Readers trust what they can visualize. Abstract claims without concrete grounding land as opinion, not insight.

**How to apply:** Patrick McKenzie's pattern in "Bits about Money": open with a real scenario (how gift-card fraud actually works), extract the principle (policy tradeoffs), then show where else it applies.

**Source:** Patrick McKenzie, "Bits about Money" — https://www.bitsaboutmoney.com. The underlying principle: "taking things well understood in particular places and exporting them to places where they are less well understood."

---

## 9. Omit Needless Words

**Principle:** Clutter delays grounding. Every word in stage-setting prose must earn its place.

**Why it matters:** Readers have finite attention. "Due to the fact that" obscures "because." "The question as to whether" muddles "whether." Clutter taxes working memory for no return.

**How to apply:** First draft: write freely. Second draft: mark every word that doesn't earn its place. In stage-setting sentences especially, every word must introduce, define, or connect.

**Sources:**
- Strunk & White, *The Elements of Style*, Rule 17 ("Omit needless words") and Rule 11 (active voice). Original Strunk 1918; White revision 1959; 4th ed. 2000
- William Zinsser, *On Writing Well* (1976, revised through 2006), Ch. 2 ("Simplicity"): "Clutter is the disease of American writing"

---

## 10. Read Aloud; Reread for Catches

**Principle:** When setup fails, the prose catches. The reader will stumble in the same places you do.

**Why it matters:** Paul Graham: "I spend far more time reading than writing. I'll reread some parts 50 or 100 times, replaying the thoughts in them and asking myself, like someone sanding a piece of wood, does anything catch?"

**How to apply:** Edit for confusion before cutting for brevity. A 2000-word essay that's clear beats a 500-word essay that's opaque. Read intros aloud—if you stumble, the reader will too.

**Sources:**
- Paul Graham, "Good Writing" (2025) — https://paulgraham.com/goodwriting.html
- William Zinsser, *On Writing Well*, "Rewriting" chapter: "Writing is thinking on paper"

---

## 11. Series as Standalone + Linked

**Principle:** Each post in a series must deliver closure on its own, while leaving breadcrumbs to the next. A new reader should finish feeling satisfied, not stranded.

**Why it matters:** Posts that require reading prior entries lose readers. Continuity shouldn't cost accessibility.

**How to apply:** Open with 1–2 sentences of prior context—a brief bridge, not an exposition dump. Close with a forward link. Each post answers a specific question fully.

**Source:** Widely held among longform technical bloggers (Simon Willison's yearend LLM roundups work this way). Summarized in: "The Guide to Creating a Series of Blog Posts" — TorchLight Hire.

## SUCCESs Framework (Supporting)

Chip & Dan Heath, *Made to Stick* (2007). Six properties of sticky ideas: **S**imple, **U**nexpected, **C**oncrete, **C**redible, **E**motional, **S**tory. Useful as a secondary check: is stage-setting not just clear, but memorable?

## Quick Reference: Which Principle for Which Problem

| Draft symptom | Principle to apply |
|---|---|
| Jargon used before defined | Progressive Disclosure (#5), Curse of Knowledge (#1) |
| "Hook" lands but reader bounces | Hook + Stakes + Skepticism (#4) |
| Reader doesn't know why to care | Reader-Centric Framing (#2), SCQA (#3) |
| Sequel post opaque to new readers | Series as Standalone + Linked (#11) |
| Intro opens with "I want to argue" | Surprise Before Argument (#6) |
| Teaches foundations first | Outcome Before Mechanism (#7) |
| All abstraction, no example | Concrete Before Abstract (#8) |
| Reader's attention drifts | Omit Needless Words (#9), Read Aloud (#10) |
| Draft reads like the writer figuring it out | Reader-Centric Framing (#2)—rewrite vertical |
